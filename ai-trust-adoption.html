AI Trust & Adoption in Health Coaching

How users build trust in AI-powered health recommendations and what makes them embrace autonomous decision-making

32 Participants Interviewed
156 Trust Factors Identified
71% AI Adoption Rate
4.2x Engagement Lift

The Challenge

As AI-powered health coaching becomes mainstream, a critical question emerges: Why do some users embrace AI recommendations while others remain skeptical? Understanding trust mechanisms is essential for designing AI products that users will actually use and rely on for health decisions.

Research Methodology

We employed a mixed-methods approach combining in-depth interviews, behavioral observation, A/B testing of transparency features, and mental model elicitation to understand how trust in AI develops and what influences adoption decisions.

👥

32 In-Depth Interviews

Users with varying health literacy, tech comfort, and AI experience. Contextual inquiry sessions observing real AI coaching interactions

🧠

Mental Model Elicitation

Drawing exercises and concept mapping to understand how users conceptualize "how AI makes decisions"

🔬

3 versions of AI explanations tested: clinical, plain language, visual reasoning. Measured adoption and follow-through

📊

Behavioral Analytics

Tracked adoption metrics, recommendation follow-through rate, and user actions after AI suggestions over 6 weeks

Key Findings

Finding 1: Trust Forms Through Predictability, Not Accuracy

Contrary to expectations, users didn't primarily judge AI trustworthiness by whether recommendations were "correct." Instead, trust developed when AI behavior was predictable and consistent. Users were more willing to trust an AI system that made clear mistakes than one that was opaque.

"I don't need it to be perfect. I just need to understand why it's suggesting something. If I can predict how it thinks, I know when to listen and when to ignore it."

Finding 2: Three Distinct User Archetypes Emerge

Users don't exist on a simple trust/skepticism spectrum. Instead, three distinct archetypes emerged, each with different trust barriers and adoption triggers.

The Delegators (28% of sample)

Profile: Quick to adopt AI, see it as helpful assistant, willing to follow suggestions

Trust barrier: Worry AI will make harmful recommendations without human oversight

Activation trigger: Human validation (coach review before implementation)

Adoption rate: 94%
Recommendation follow-through: 78%

The Skeptics (37% of sample)

Profile: Slow to trust, want to understand "why," prefer AI as idea generator not decision-maker

Trust barrier: Black box algorithms feel untrustworthy; fear of losing control

Activation trigger: Detailed explainability + ability to override/customize

Adoption rate: 58%
Recommendation follow-through: 32%

The Contextual Users (35% of sample)

Profile: Trust varies by context; high trust for fitness, low trust for medical decisions

Trust barrier: Domain-specific concerns (medical legitimacy, credential verification)

Activation trigger: Evidence of expertise + peer/expert endorsement

Adoption rate: 72%
Recommendation follow-through: 51%

Finding 3: Transparency ≠ One-Size-Fits-All

A/B testing revealed that explaining AI recommendations isn't universally effective. The format, depth, and type of explanation dramatically impacted adoption.

Three Transparency Approaches Tested:

Approach Example Adoption Rate User Satisfaction Best For
Clinical "Based on VO2max analysis and recovery patterns" 41% 5.2/10 Health professionals
Plain Language "Your body needs easier workouts this week. You haven't fully recovered yet" 68% 7.1/10 General population
Visual Reasoning Graph showing energy levels vs. workout intensity with arrow pointing to recommendation 76% 8.2/10 Visual learners, quick decisions

Key insight: Visual reasoning outperformed both other methods, particularly for Skeptics and Contextual Users who wanted quick understanding without overwhelming detail.

Finding 4: Mental Model Mismatch Creates Friction

Users held significantly inaccurate mental models of how AI makes health recommendations. This mismatch directly correlated with low adoption and mistrust.

Common Misconceptions:

  • "AI knows my full medical history" (Reality: It only sees data you've provided) — Led 43% of users to worry about accuracy without realizing information gaps
  • "AI learns from every user, not just me" (Reality: Some models personalize) — Created expectations of hyper-personalization that weren't met
  • "AI is always improving" (Reality: Static models without updates) — Caused users to expect better recommendations over time, then distrust when they didn't improve
  • "AI has emotions/intentions" (Reality: Algorithmic pattern matching) — Led users to anthropomorphize, creating unrealistic expectations
Users with corrected mental models showed 2.8x higher adoption rates and 3.1x better recommendation follow-through

Finding 5: Human Oversight Paradoxically Increases AI Trust

Counterintuitively, adding human review (a coach or health professional reviewing AI suggestions) increased AI adoption by 34%, even though it slowed implementation.

Why Human Oversight Builds Trust:

  • Creates "safety net" feeling — Users know a human will catch mistakes
  • Demonstrates organizational confidence — "If our coaches trust this, it must be reliable"
  • Provides legitimacy through credential stacking — AI + human expertise feels more credible
  • Reduces decision burden — Users don't have to judge AI quality; they trust the human
With human oversight: 71% adoption
Without oversight: 53% adoption

Recommendations & Implementation Strategy

Based on these findings, we recommend a differentiated approach that segments users by trust archetype and provides customized transparency and oversight strategies.

Key Outcomes

  • Trust is built through transparency and predictability, not accuracy. Users are more forgiving of wrong recommendations if they understand the reasoning.
  • User segmentation matters. One-size-fits-all AI explanations fail; customizing by user archetype dramatically improves adoption.
  • Mental models are critical intervention points. Correcting false assumptions about how AI works led to 2.8x adoption improvements.
  • Human oversight is a trust amplifier. Adding human review increased adoption by 34% despite adding friction.
  • Visual reasoning outperforms text. Even for health-literate users, visual explanations drove higher adoption and satisfaction than clinical language.
  • Context matters more than domain. Users trusted AI more for fitness than medical decisions, but this varied by individual context and experience.

Limitations & Future Research

Research Limitations

  • Sample focused on health/fitness context; may not generalize to other domains
  • Relatively tech-savvy population (all had smartphones); older adults may show different patterns
  • Single AI system tested; different AI models/interfaces may produce different results
  • 6-week study duration; long-term trust dynamics unknown
  • Did not test across cultural contexts; trust triggers may differ by culture

Future Research Directions

  • Longitudinal study tracking trust evolution over 6+ months
  • Cross-cultural comparison of AI trust factors
  • Testing with older adults and health-anxious populations
  • Exploring trust in different AI domains (financial, legal, creative)
  • Impact of AI failures on trust recovery and rebuilding
  • Measuring unconscious trust signals beyond self-report
