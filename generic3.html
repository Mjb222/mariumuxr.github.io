User research, competitive analysis and testing the MVP of a mental health ap﻿p

Project Overview

My Role: User Researcher
Methods: Desk Research, UX Audit, Competitive Analysis, Survey, Usability & A/B Testing, Thematic Analysis
Tools: Figma, Otter, pen & paper
Duration: Jan 2020 2022 - Feb 2021

Goal

Flow developed a mental health app to help people learn social and emotional skills to cope with life challenges. They approached me to evaluate their MVP, learn more about prospective users, find a way to keep learners engaged and motivated, and recommend UXR based insights to optimize the app's performance.

UXR Strategy

By conducting a comprehensive study involving surveys, interviews, and usability testing, I acquired valuable insights into the preferences and concerns of prospective users. This process enabled me to identify significant usability challenges within the current iteration of the application. Moreover, I undertook an in-depth analysis of other competitors in mental health industry to explore effective strategies for enhancing user engagement and retention. Subsequently, I formulated design recommendations based on these findings to optimize the app's performance and user experience.

Understanding the Product Vision

Flow app’s researchers had conducted a small research with family friends beta testers and had following assumptions about their users:
- Users between 13-19 years old
-- Clinical (people with mental health condition diagnosis) & non-clinical people
-- Interested in self-improvement
-- Mental health advocates
Before conducting UXR, I dedicated time to understanding the role early prevention (social & emotional skills) that plays an important role in preventing mental health disorders and improve one’s mental health. As a psychologist, I already had deep understanding of significance and how implementation of various therapeutic approaches in early prevention works. Therefore, along with Flow app’s team, three main objectives for the research were decided upon:
- Discover the strengths and weaknesses of the current MVP
-- Understand how easily users are interacting with our application
-- Understand what actions users take while using our application
-- Understand how they feel about the design and colors
- Learn more about users
-- Learn about competitor applications our users are using
- Identify opportunities for users to stay interested and engaged
-- Take feedback from users about improvements we can implement

Survey

To understand beta testers experience with the Flow app, I created a survey to learn more about them and their experience with the app. I focused on following factors:
-- How do they feel about colors & design
-- Their opinion about features in the app
-- How actively they use mental health apps
-- Information on their wellness habits
-- How is their experience using Flow app?

Unfortunately, only 7 beta testers responded and insights I got from them was not very insight and nobody was willing to take part in a user interview. However, in addition to sending surveys to beta testers, I also shared survey on various social media groups for mental health improvement to learn more about their wellness habits. Social media survey received 86 responses and over 61% indicated that they were actively using some wellness app or programs. 14.2% responded about their wellness habits. 18% gave their opinion about the design and colors of the app.

Competitive Landscape: Understanding & Insights

In the survey that I created for Flow app, I also asked about other mental health apps or wellness app participants were using. I also asked about the other mental health or wellness apps that people were currently using.
-- 61% were already using wellness app
-- Headspace and BetterHelp were two famous apps that were used on the weekly basis by the participants.
To probe more into participants’ opinion about app they were using, I asked what they liked and disliked about these apps and what features standout for them. This question helped out and I recognized some clear themes through thematic analysis. Some important findings that I was able to extract were:
-- Usability - app was easy to use and navigate
-- People could track their progress
-- Bright colors of apps gave feeling of hopefulness and optimism.
- Features participants disliked were:
-- Very basic UI with no emojis or engaging graphics, which makes participants uninterested in the app easily
To develop a deeper understanding of the competitive landscape, I conducted a competitor analysis. I compared BetterHelp and Headspace as Flow app indicated these two apps as their main competitors. Furthermore, few survey participants/respondents also indicated that they were using one or the other.
During my desk research, I examined that these both apps had good user retention as per rating on the app stores. Thus, i focused on examining their user retention and engagement strategy to understand their value proposition. I also looked at mental health communities, feedback on app store, and blogs on the respective competitor apps to understand pain points of users and design opportunities for optimization.


Competitive Analysis

During competitive analysis, I identified following strategies for user engagement, retention, and app optimization:
- Onboarding process explanation for great first time user experience
- Daily encouragement notifications
- Gaming aspect- Streaks
- Awards for achieving a certain task
- Encouragement after tasks completion
- Community engagement
- In-app feedback option

Current Solution: Evaluation

UX DESIGN AUDIT

Navigation effect on user engagement: The navigation for various mental health audio courses was hidden in the “Training” tab , which was making it difficult for user to reach them. Users indicated that they preferred to listen to courses like a podcast while doing other tasks instead of making time to read through the courses visually.

Usability issues: Participants thought the app was a little confusing because they had difficulty understanding the meaning of a few widgets. For example, the “chat icon” was used for “upcoming workshop” feature, which created confusion because users thought they could chat or join a community with the help of chat icon widget. Moreover, the app was reported as bit slow.

Accessibility, the color of toolbar section was reported as not visually appealing and color contrasts were bit off. On several occasions, the chosen color palette for the app was not properly aligned with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG). Having sufficient color contrast is crucial for people with color blindness as well as it is also important for an app that people use while they're outside in the sun or in well-lit places. In those situations, the difference between colors can already be hard to see.

Interview

As UX design audit is a very useful tools and helps uncover tricky areas of the product. Usability testing provides insight into in what ways people are utilizing and engaging with the product. For understand more in-depth insights I recurited 4 participants who filled out the survey earlier for an in-depth interview combined with the remote usability test. All participants had different motivations and goals for using wellness app but their difficulties were quite similar. They struggled with the length of the courses and reported them as too long. This resulted into lost interest. Also, no reward was proposed after completing a specific course which lowered the motivation more. The font size and styles were also not liked by the users, which made app less appealing to use.

Usability Test

AAfter conducting in-depth interview, I utilized remote usability test. I asked participants to explore the MVP as they would do normally and think aloud. The feedback was very help and included positive feedback and suggestions on which areas to improve.
Participants found following features as likeable and creative:
- Quizes
- Meditation Feature
- Daily tips
- Audio feature of courses
- Digital Journaling
Nevertheless, I also observed various usability issues, and jotted them down in a spreadsheet. The following issues needed to be addressed:
- Quizzes were not timed
- Some words like “Dialectic” and “reinforcing” were used unclearly in the courses.
- The tracker for previous journal entries were not working.
- There were no images for practicing various confidence poses.
- Participants couldn’t see their progress on quizzes and courses.
- the “chat icon” was used for “upcoming workshop” feature, which created confusion because users thought they could chat or join a community with the help of chat icon widget.

A/B Testing

Finally, I did A/B testing using Figma as Flow app had colors options for various screens. The colors palettes they had still didn’t meet the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).

Recommendations

After conducting a through research and according to my findings, I proposed following changes:
- Create an in-app feedback system so that users can communicate their expectations or problems they are facing.
- Use easy vocabulary instead of advanced
- Prepare onboarding video for users this will increase value proposition
- Use widgets that have common meanings globally
- Use good color contrast that meet Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)
- Moreover, I proposed that they should make a community forum in their app as well where people can connect with other people going through same journey. This appeared in one the confusion with the widget but this sounds like something people were expecting when they saw the chat icon.

Impact

- This track of UXR has left a strong legacy, and a comprehensive playbook for the company to leverage to improve app features.
- I made sketches for suggestions and through collaboration with design and dev team we were able to quickly relay feedback from research and incorporate it to the prototype - Agile!
